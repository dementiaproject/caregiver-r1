## 6. Therapeutic Fibbing (Day et al., 2011; Schermer, 2007)

**Clinical context**: Compassionate non-truth-telling, ethically debated. Schermer (2007) argues lying is *prima facie* wrong but can sometimes be justified by appeal to wellbeing when capacity to be deceived has diminished. Surveys report ~96% of professional caregivers use it situationally.

**When it tends to help**: Confronting a false belief would cause severe and avoidable distress, AND there is no safety cost to going along, AND the person can no longer reliably hold the corrected fact.

**When NOT to use**: The person is calm and asking a sincere factual question; the correction matters for safety (medication, dosing, identity in calm state); the deception would need to be elaborate or repeated. Hard rule 3 forbids using this for routine convenience.

**Core procedure**:
- Enter the person's experienced reality without affirming the literal false fact
- Keep the response minimal, not elaborately fabricated
- Pair with a calming present-tense action

**Example phrasing**: "She's not here just now, but let's wait together — would you like a cup of tea while we sit?"
